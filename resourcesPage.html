FAQ's

What are the main Symptoms of diabetes?

  • Being thirstier than usual
  • Needing to pee more.
  • Feeling tired and having no energy
  • Always feeling hungry
  • Having cuts that heal slowly.
  • Itching, skin infections
  • Blurred vision
  • Mood swings
  • Headaches
  • Feeling dizzy
  • Leg Cramps

    • For type 1, how is diabetes managed?

    Insulin injections are needed daily, along with a healthy lifestyle and diet.

    Is there a cure for diabetes?

    There is a huge amount of research being done both in Australia and globally, however now there is no cure.

    How many people in Australia are suffering from type 1 or type 2 diabetes?

    Currently 127,000 Australians are currently living with type 1 diabetes, while 1.2 million Australians are currently diagnosed with type 2 diabetes, with a further 500,000 being currently undiagnosed.

    What is the difference between Hypoglycaemia, and Hyperglycemia?

  • Hypoglycaemia, is when the blood sugar level is less than 4.0mmol/L (millimoles per litre of blood), this is when the blood sugar level is too low. Its causes can be, because of too much insulin, or not eating enough. It is important to recognize quickly as it may cause the individual to faint, fall into a coma or may cause the brain to function incorrectly.
  • Hyperglycemia, is when the blood sugar level is greater than 11.1mmol/L, this is when the blood sugar level is too high. It can be caused by eating too much, not using enough insulin, stress or other disease. Extra insulin is required.     • What is the difference between Type 1 & Type 2 diabetes?

    Type 1
  • Is not caused by lifestyle, and currently has no known causes or cure.
  • Type 1 Diabetes is often diagnosed in childhood. It can however, occur at any age
  • Requires an ongoing insulin therapy treatment.
  • It occurs after the cells in the pancreas have been destroyed by the body's immune system. This results in the body being unable to produce any insulin

    • Type 2
  • Type 2 Diabetes represents 85-90% of all diabetes cases. This number is increasing every year.
  • Risk factors for Type 2 Diabetes include:
    • Family History
    • Ethnicity
    • Age
  • Lifestyle factors include:
    • Unhealthy diet
    • Lack of physical exercise
  • Type 2 diabetes can be delayed or prevented in changes to diet and lifestyle in almost 60% of cases.
  • Type 2 Diabetes occurs when either the insulin that is produced isn't working effectively, or if the pancreas is not producing enough insulin.

    • What are the target blood glucose levels for both Type 1 & Type 2 diabetes.

    Normal blood glucose levels are between 4.0-7.8mmol/L.

    Type 1 Diabetes

  • Target Levels:
    • 4-8mmol/L before meals
    • >10mmol/L two hours after starting meals

    • Type 2 Diabetes

  • Target Levels:
    • 6-8mmol/L before meals
    • 6-10mmol/L two hours after starting meals
